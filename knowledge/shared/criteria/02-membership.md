# Criterion 2: Membership in Associations

## Legal Standard
The beneficiary must hold membership in associations that require outstanding achievements, as judged by recognized national or international experts. Three sub-elements: (1) current membership, (2) outstanding achievement required for admission, (3) admission judged by recognized experts.

USCIS evaluates all three sub-elements separately. Missing any one triggers an RFE.

## Evidence Hierarchy (strongest to weakest)
1. Signed letter from the organization to USCIS stating acceptance rate, criteria, and decision-makers
2. Official acceptance email/letter with competitive language ("after reviewing X applications")
3. Organization's website showing published criteria + named decision-makers with credentials
4. Expert profiles of people who make admission decisions
5. Organization's constitution, bylaws, or charter documenting membership criteria
6. Membership agreement/executed contract (proves membership, not selectivity)
7. Screenshots of member profile on platform (corroborating only)

## Winning Argument Patterns

### VC Accelerator/Fellowship as Membership
Accelerators are accepted as "associations" when:
- Framed as a selective community, not just an investment
- Acceptance rate or applicant count is cited
- Decision-makers are named with credentials
- Benefits include community access, not just money

### Invite-Only Professional Councils
- Preempt "pay-to-play" objection: cite acceptance rate, show fee alone doesn't guarantee admission
- Emphasize the expert review committee and its credentials
- Request a USCIS support letter directly from the organization

### Technical/Professional Societies
Strong when admission criteria are objective and publicly documented with a multi-step process (application, recommendation, interview, payment).

## Membership Argument Framework
For each membership, answer at least 4 of these 7 with sourced facts:

| Question | Source |
|----------|--------|
| Acceptance rate or applicant:accepted ratio? | Org website, USCIS letter |
| Total member count or cohort size? | Org website |
| Notable members/alumni? | Org website, press |
| Who decides admission and their credentials? | Org website, expert profiles |
| Exact admission requirements? | Org website, acceptance letter |
| Geographic reach (countries, regions)? | Org website |
| Founding year? | Org website |

If you cannot answer at least 4, the membership argument is weak.

## Best Practices
- Always prove all three sub-elements independently: membership, outstanding achievement requirement, expert judgment
- A financial threshold alone (e.g., "$500K revenue required") is NOT "outstanding achievement" — show additional qualitative expert review beyond the financial bar
- Every membership must be explicitly connected to the beneficiary's stated field of endeavor
- Include governing documents (bylaws, charter) proactively — USCIS may request them
- DAOs and token-gated communities are NOT qualifying associations — membership must involve human expert judgment
- Listing decision-maker names is not enough — include bios with specific experience, titles, credentials
- Always include membership proof alongside selectivity evidence — proving the org is selective doesn't prove the beneficiary is a member
- For new organizations (< 2 years), cite member count, external press, and notable alumni to counter "created to support the petition" concern
