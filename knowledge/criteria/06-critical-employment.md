# Criterion 6: Critical Employment at Distinguished Organizations

## Legal Standard
Evidence that the beneficiary has been employed in a critical or essential capacity for organizations or establishments that have a distinguished reputation. Two sub-elements: (1) critical or essential capacity, (2) distinguished reputation of the organization.

## Evidence Hierarchy

### For Critical Capacity (strongest to weakest):
1. Expert/employer letter from a senior leader detailing specific contributions and measurable outcomes
2. Employment verification letter from HR listing specific duties and accomplishments
3. Offer letter showing title, compensation, and equity
4. Performance reviews or promotion records
5. Resume/CV (weakest — self-reported)

### For Distinguished Reputation (strongest to weakest):
1. Press coverage of the organization with circulation figures
2. VC funding/valuation from recognized investors
3. Industry rankings (Forbes lists, Gartner reports)
4. Notable partnerships (Fortune 500 contracts, government agencies)
5. Organization's own website (weakest — self-serving)

## What "Critical" Means
USCIS looks for:
- **Specific, named contributions** — "Built the data processing pipeline" not "contributed to engineering"
- **Measurable impact** — "Doubled SLA performance" not "improved performance"
- **Irreplaceability** — Would the organization have suffered if this person left?
- **Seniority or scope** — Senior/lead roles are easier to argue than junior roles

## Letter Requirements
Every support letter must contain:
- Author's name, address, and title
- How they know the beneficiary and their work (personal knowledge basis)
- Detailed, specific information about the beneficiary's role
- How the role is/was critical within the organization
- Specific tasks and accomplishments compared to others in similar roles

## Best Practices
- Title alone proves nothing — a "Software Engineer" title requires a letter explaining why that specific role was critical
- Always pair the title with specific metrics, scope of responsibility, and measurable outcomes
- Founder/CEO claims require corporate documentation: articles of incorporation, business licenses, board resolutions — a resume is NOT sufficient
- Organization's "distinguished reputation" must be established with external evidence — size and longevity alone are not determinative
- Support letter authors must demonstrate personal knowledge of the beneficiary's work — generic praise without specifics is discounted
- Reconcile any title discrepancies between documents (resume vs. HR docs) before filing
- For startups, establish distinguished reputation through funding/valuation, investor names, press coverage, and industry position
- Get letters from people with direct, firsthand knowledge — not someone relying on hearsay
