# O-1A: Extraordinary Ability in Sciences, Business, or Education

## Regulatory Basis
- **Statute:** INA § 101(a)(15)(O)(i)
- **Regulation:** 8 CFR § 214.2(o)
- **Standard:** The beneficiary must demonstrate "extraordinary ability in the sciences, arts, education, business, or athletics which has been demonstrated by sustained national or international acclaim."

## What "Extraordinary Ability" Means
Per USCIS Policy Manual: "a level of expertise indicating that the individual is one of that small percentage who has risen to the very top of the field of endeavor."

This is the same standard as EB-1A but for a nonimmigrant (temporary) visa.

## Burden of Proof
- **Standard:** Preponderance of evidence — "more likely than not"
- **Two-step analysis (Kazarian framework):**
  1. Step 1: Does the evidence meet the plain language of at least 3 of the 8 criteria?
  2. Step 2: Does the totality of the evidence demonstrate sustained national/international acclaim and extraordinary ability?

## The 8 Criteria (must meet at least 3)

| # | Criterion | Short Name |
|---|-----------|------------|
| 1 | Nationally or internationally recognized prizes or awards for excellence | Awards |
| 2 | Membership in associations requiring outstanding achievements, judged by experts | Membership |
| 3 | Published material in major trade publications or major media about the beneficiary | Published Material |
| 4 | Original scientific, scholarly, or business-related contributions of major significance | Original Contributions |
| 5 | Authorship of scholarly articles in professional journals or major media | Scholarly Articles |
| 6 | Employment in a critical or essential capacity at distinguished organizations | Critical Employment |
| 7 | Commanded a high salary or other significantly high remuneration | High Remuneration |
| 8 | Participation as a judge of the work of others in the field | Judging |

## How Adjudicators Actually Read O-1A Petitions

1. **They skim.** The petition may be 200+ pages. The adjudicator reads the support letter first, then spot-checks exhibits. If the support letter doesn't clearly state why each criterion is met, the exhibits won't save it.

2. **They look for the 3 easiest criteria first.** If 3 criteria are clearly met at Step 1, they move to Step 2. They don't need to be impressed by all 8 — just convinced by 3.

3. **Step 2 is where most denials happen.** Even if you technically meet 3 criteria, the adjudicator asks: "Is this person really at the top of their field?" Weak criteria (e.g., judging a small local event) can pass Step 1 but hurt you at Step 2.

4. **They compare against the field.** "Top of the field" is relative. A tech entrepreneur's field is other tech entrepreneurs, not all businesspeople. Define the field narrowly enough that the beneficiary stands out.

5. **They are skeptical of self-serving evidence.** Expert letters from friends/colleagues get less weight than letters from people with no relationship to the beneficiary. Third-party evidence (press, awards from independent orgs) gets more weight than first-party evidence (the beneficiary's own pitch deck).

## Common Mistakes

1. **Too many weak criteria instead of fewer strong ones.** 3 strong criteria > 6 weak ones. Adjudicators see through padding.
2. **Field of endeavor too broad.** "Technology" is too broad. "AI-driven consumer health technology" is specific enough to show the beneficiary is at the top.
3. **No numbers.** "Prestigious organization" means nothing without acceptance rate, member count, or ranking.
4. **Expert letters that all say the same thing.** Each letter should address different criteria or different aspects of the beneficiary's work.
5. **Ignoring Step 2.** Meeting 3 criteria technically doesn't guarantee approval. The totality must show sustained acclaim.
