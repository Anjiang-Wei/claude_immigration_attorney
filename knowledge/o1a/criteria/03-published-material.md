# Criterion 3: Published Material About the Beneficiary

## Legal Standard
"Published material in professional or major trade publications or major media about the beneficiary, relating to the beneficiary's work in the field for which classification is sought."

### Key Distinction
This is about material written ABOUT the beneficiary by others. Articles the beneficiary wrote go under Criterion 5 (Scholarly Articles).

### Three Sub-Elements
1. **Published material** — actually published (not just a blog post by the beneficiary)
2. **In major trade publications or major media** — the outlet must be "major" (circulation matters)
3. **About the beneficiary and their work** — must be about them specifically, not just a mention

## Evidence Hierarchy (strongest → weakest)

1. **Feature profile of the beneficiary** in a national/international outlet with named author and circulation data
2. **Article substantially about the beneficiary's company/product** with the beneficiary quoted by name
3. **Industry publication coverage** with circulation figures (SimilarWeb, press kit, Alexa)
4. **News article mentioning the beneficiary** in context of a larger story (weaker — is it really "about" them?)
5. **Syndicated/reposted articles** (same article on multiple outlets — counts as one publication with broader reach)
6. **Blog posts or self-published content** (generally not accepted unless the platform is itself a "major" publication)

## What Makes a Publication "Major"?

| Factor | Strong | Weak |
|--------|--------|------|
| Circulation/traffic | 1M+ monthly unique visitors | < 100K monthly visitors |
| Editorial process | Staff editors, submission review | Self-publish, no editorial gate |
| Industry recognition | Known by name in the field | Obscure, no industry reputation |
| Geographic reach | National or international readership | Local only |
| Examples | Forbes, TechCrunch, 36Kr, Nature, IEEE | Random WordPress blogs, press release wire services |

**Press release distribution services** (PR Newswire, BusinessWire) are generally NOT "major media" — they are paid distribution. But if a real outlet picks up the story and publishes it editorially, that version counts.

## Things to Watch For

> These are structural weaknesses to think about when building the argument — not confirmed RFE patterns based on real case data.

| Trigger | Why It Gets RFE'd | How to Prevent |
|---------|-------------------|----------------|
| No circulation figures | Adjudicator can't assess if outlet is "major" | Always include monthly unique visitors from SimilarWeb or the outlet's press kit |
| Article is a press release, not editorial | Paid placement ≠ published material | Distinguish between press releases and editorial coverage; only claim editorial pieces |
| Article mentions beneficiary but isn't "about" them | A list of 50 startups mentioning the beneficiary is a mention, not coverage | Focus on articles where the beneficiary is the subject or a primary focus |
| Outlet is not in English and adjudicator can't assess it | Foreign-language publications need context | Provide certified translation + evidence the outlet is "major" in its market |
| All articles are from the same media market | Domestic-only coverage | Include at least one international outlet or explain the outlet's international readership |
