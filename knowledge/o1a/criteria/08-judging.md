# Criterion 8: Judging the Work of Others

## Legal Standard
"Evidence of the beneficiary's participation, either on a panel, or individually, as a judge of the work of others in the same or an allied field of specification for which classification is sought."

### Sub-Elements
1. **Participation as a judge** — the beneficiary actually evaluated others' work
2. **In the same or allied field** — the judging must relate to the beneficiary's field

## Evidence Hierarchy (strongest → weakest)

1. **Formal judge confirmation letter** on the event organizer's letterhead — names the beneficiary, describes judging responsibilities
2. **Invitation email** from the organizer specifically inviting the beneficiary to judge
3. **Completed score sheets / judging rubrics** showing the beneficiary actually scored submissions
4. **Public listing as a judge** on event website or Devpost page (screenshot)
5. **Photos at the event** in a judging capacity (weakest — corroborating only)

## What Makes a Judging Event Strong vs. Weak

| Factor | Strong | Weak |
|--------|--------|------|
| Event prestige | Stanford TreeHacks, Stevie Awards, UN-affiliated programs | Unnamed Discord hackathon with 10 participants |
| Scale | 1,000+ participants, $200K+ prizes | < 50 participants, no prizes |
| Selection to judge | Formal invitation based on expertise | Self-applied, anyone could judge |
| Scope | International (multiple countries) | Single classroom or campus |
| Evidence | Letterhead confirmation + score sheets | Only a screenshot of a Slack message |
| Relevance to field | AI/tech hackathon for a "Technology Entrepreneurship" beneficiary | Unrelated field (cooking competition for a tech entrepreneur) |

## Strategy: Lead with Quality, Stack with Volume

Don't treat all judging events equally. Structure the argument in tiers:

### Tier 1: Full argument paragraphs (2-3 events)
Pick the 2-3 most prestigious events. Write a full argument paragraph for each with:
- Event name, date, organizer
- **Sourced statistics:** number of participants, acceptance rate, prize amount, countries represented
- **Evidence of actual judging:** score sheets, confirmation letter
- Why the event is significant in the field

### Tier 2: Summary table (remaining events)
List all other events in a table showing volume. Don't write full paragraphs — just prove they happened:

| Event | Date | Evidence |
|-------|------|----------|
| [Name] | [Date] | [Exhibit reference] |

### Closing paragraph
Tie it together: "In total, the Beneficiary served as a judge at [N]+ events between [date range], evaluating the work of others in [field]."

**Why this structure works:** The Tier 1 events prove quality and international recognition. The summary table proves volume and sustained pattern. Together they satisfy both Step 1 and Step 2 of the Kazarian analysis.

## Things to Watch For

> These are structural weaknesses to think about when building the argument — not confirmed RFE patterns based on real case data.

| Trigger | Why It Gets RFE'd | How to Prevent |
|---------|-------------------|----------------|
| Events are too small or obscure | "Not shown that this constitutes judging at a level consistent with extraordinary ability" | Lead with the strongest events; include participant count and prestige markers |
| No evidence of actual judging | Having an invitation ≠ having judged | Include score sheets, completed rubrics, or post-event confirmation |
| Events not in the beneficiary's field | Judging a cooking competition doesn't help a tech petition | Explain how each event relates to the field (most hackathons are clearly tech) |
| All events are from the same organizer | Looks like one relationship, not broad recognition | Diversify: university hackathons + business awards + accelerator mentoring + conferences |
| Only self-applied judging roles | "The beneficiary sought out these roles rather than being recognized" | Include events where the beneficiary was invited/recruited, not just events where they applied |

## Mentoring and Speaking as Judging

### Techstars / Accelerator Mentoring
- **Argument:** Mentoring startup founders involves evaluating their work, providing critical feedback, and guiding strategic decisions — this constitutes "judging the work of others"
- **Strength:** Techstars is globally recognized; being on the mentor roster implies expert status
- **Weakness:** "Mentoring" is not the same word as "judging" — be explicit about the evaluative nature

### Conference Speaking
- **Argument:** Invited speakers evaluate and present expert analysis of developments in the field
- **Strength:** Shows the beneficiary is recognized as an authority
- **Weakness:** Weaker than direct judging — speaking ≠ evaluating others' work. Use as corroboration, not primary evidence.
